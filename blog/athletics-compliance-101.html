Athletics Compliance 101: What Every College Athlete Should Know

Compliance By Jay Haws February 8, 2026 9 min read

The NCAA Bylaws are over 400 pages long. Nobody expects college athletes to read all of them. But the rules that most directly affect your eligibility—your ability to compete, your scholarship, your career—can usually be summarized in far less. This post is that summary.

I work in the Wake Forest Athletics compliance office as an extern, and the questions I hear most often from athletes aren't about obscure technicalities. They're about the things everyone kind of knows matter but nobody explained clearly. Let me try to fix that.

Eligibility: The Foundation

Your eligibility is your ticket to compete. Lose it and you sit—or worse, your team forfeits games retroactively. Eligibility is governed by several overlapping requirements:

Academic Requirements

NCAA Division I athletes must meet initial eligibility standards (GPA and test score requirements for incoming freshmen) and then satisfy continuing eligibility standards each year:

  • You must be enrolled full-time (usually 12 credits per semester).
  • You must complete at least 40% of your degree requirements by the end of your second year, 60% by end of year three, 80% by end of year four.
  • You must maintain satisfactory academic progress as defined by your institution.
  • You must declare a major by the beginning of your third year.

Missing any of these milestones can trigger an eligibility issue. Your compliance office tracks this, but you should track it too.

Seasons of Competition

In most sports, you have four seasons of competition available within a five-year window from your initial enrollment. Understanding how seasons and years of eligibility are counted matters enormously when you're considering redshirting, transferring, or taking time away from competition.

NIL: A Rapidly Evolving Landscape

Since 2021, athletes can profit from their Name, Image, and Likeness. But NIL has compliance dimensions that aren't always obvious.

  • Disclosure requirements: Most schools require you to disclose NIL deals before or shortly after signing. Know your school's specific policy—the timing and process varies.
  • Booster involvement: NIL deals with school boosters or booster-funded collectives are subject to heightened scrutiny. The line between legitimate NIL and impermissible pay-for-play runs through these arrangements.
  • Pay-for-play prohibition: NIL compensation cannot be conditioned on your athletic performance or on a specific enrollment decision. "We'll pay you X if you sign with us" is still illegal.
  • No school trademark use: You can say you play for your school, but you cannot use the school's logo, colors (as trademarks), or other protected marks without separate authorization.

The safe answer whenever you're unsure: ask compliance before you sign.

Agent Rules

This is one of the most misunderstood areas of compliance, and it has changed significantly in recent years.

Under current NCAA rules, college athletes can generally work with licensed agents for NIL purposes. However, working with a sports agent in connection with your professional athletic career—contract negotiations, draft preparation, scouting services—can still trigger eligibility complications depending on the sport and circumstances.

Key principles:

  • An agent cannot provide you with money, gifts, or benefits.
  • An agent's representation of you in connection with professional leagues must be handled carefully to avoid jeopardizing remaining eligibility.
  • Make sure any agent you work with is properly licensed (registered with the appropriate state licensing authority and player's association where applicable).
  • Get the terms of any representation agreement in writing and have a professional review it before signing.

The Transfer Portal

The transfer portal has fundamentally changed college athletics. Athletes now have far more freedom to transfer and compete immediately, but there are still rules that matter.

Entering the Portal

Entering the transfer portal is a significant decision. Once you enter, your current institution can reduce or revoke your athletic scholarship depending on your sport and scholarship terms. Understand what your scholarship agreement says before you enter.

Immediate Eligibility

Most transfers now have immediate eligibility at their new school under the one-time transfer exception. But eligibility is not automatic—you must satisfy academic requirements, there are windows for when you can transfer and remain eligible for postseason play, and some sports have additional rules.

Communication

While you're in the portal, coaches from other schools can contact you directly. But schools cannot contact athletes who have not entered the portal—athletes can initiate that contact, but coaches initiating contact with athletes still enrolled at another school (outside of the portal) is an NCAA violation.

Extra Benefits: The Most Common Trap

"Extra benefits" violations are perhaps the most common compliance issues athletes face—and they're often completely innocent mistakes.

An extra benefit is anything you receive based on your status as a student-athlete that isn't available to the general student body under the same terms.

This includes things that seem harmless: a free meal from a restaurant that sponsors the athletics program, gear from a booster, discounts offered specifically because you're an athlete. These are all potentially impermissible extra benefits, even when the person offering them means well.

The safest rule: if someone is offering you something because you play a sport, and it's not going through your athletic department, check with compliance first.

What the Compliance Office Is Actually There to Do

I want to close with this, because I think athletes often have a distorted view of what compliance offices do. We are not there to catch you doing something wrong. We are there to help you stay eligible so you can keep doing what you love.

The athletes who get in trouble with compliance are almost never the ones who asked questions. They're the ones who assumed they knew the answer, or assumed the risk was low, or didn't want to seem like they were doing something wrong by asking.

Ask anyway. The compliance office is confidential, the conversation is free, and the alternative— losing eligibility over something avoidable—is devastating.

The rules governing college athletics are genuinely complex, and they're changing faster than ever. Build a relationship with your compliance staff early. Use them as a resource. That's what they're for.
