Building Your NIL Brand: A Practical Guide for College Athletes

Sports Law By Jay Haws April 22, 2026 7 min read

When NIL went into effect in July 2021, the most common question I heard from athletes was simple: "Can I do this?" Five years later, the question has evolved: "How do I do this right?"

As an Athletics Compliance extern at Wake Forest and a law student focused on sports law, I've watched a lot of athletes navigate NIL well—and a fair number stumble into problems that were entirely avoidable. This guide is an attempt to share what I've learned from both sides.

What NIL Actually Allows

"NIL" stands for Name, Image, and Likeness. Since 2021, NCAA rules have allowed college athletes to profit from their own NIL through sponsorship deals, social media partnerships, appearances, autograph signings, camps, merchandise, and more.

What NIL does not allow—at least under the rules as they currently stand—is direct payment for athletic performance. You can be paid to post about a brand on Instagram. You cannot be paid to win games. That distinction matters, and it's where a lot of shady NIL structures fall apart under scrutiny.

The Foundation: Your Personal Brand

The athletes who monetize NIL most effectively don't chase deals—they build something worth paying for. That means:

  • A consistent online presence. You don't need to be on every platform. Pick one or two and be consistent. Brands want to see engaged audiences, not just follower counts.
  • A clear identity. Are you the athlete who talks about faith and leadership? The one who documents training? The one with the dry sense of humor and a knack for cooking? The specificity is what makes you marketable.
  • Content that adds value. The most sustainable NIL earners are creators first and athletes second. Think about what you know that others want to learn.

Deal Structures to Know

NIL deals come in several common forms. Understanding the structure of each helps you negotiate better and avoid getting taken advantage of.

Flat Fee Deals

You're paid a fixed amount for a specific deliverable—one Instagram post, one appearance, one video. This is the most common structure for emerging NIL earners. The advantage is predictability; the disadvantage is that you're leaving money on the table if the content outperforms expectations.

Royalty or Commission Deals

You're paid based on sales or conversions driven by your content or code. Higher upside, but also higher risk—you can put in significant effort and see little return if the brand's product doesn't resonate with your audience.

Equity Deals

Some NIL arrangements involve equity stakes in companies, particularly startups. These are high-risk, high-reward, and require careful legal review. Don't accept equity without having someone look at the cap table and the term sheet.

Collective Arrangements

Athletic collectives pool resources from boosters and fans to pay athletes for NIL activities. These arrangements exist in a legally complex space and are subject to increasing scrutiny. Proceed with awareness, not just enthusiasm.

The Tax Reality

This is the part most athletes aren't prepared for, and it can create real financial problems if you ignore it.

NIL income is taxable. All of it. If you earn more than $600 from a single source, you'll receive a 1099 form. Even if you don't, you're still legally required to report the income.

Because most NIL deals treat athletes as independent contractors rather than employees, you're also responsible for self-employment taxes—which means you're paying both the employee and employer portions of Social Security and Medicare, a combined rate of 15.3% on top of your regular income tax.

A few practical steps:

  • Set aside 25-30% of every NIL payment for taxes from the start.
  • Consider making quarterly estimated tax payments if you're earning regularly.
  • Track every business expense related to your NIL activity—equipment, software, travel to appearances, even a portion of your phone bill. These are potentially deductible.
  • Work with a CPA or tax professional who has experience with athlete NIL income. This isn't the place for DIY.

Compliance: Don't Skip This Part

Most schools require athletes to disclose NIL deals to the compliance office—either before signing or shortly after. The specific rules vary by school and state, but the general principle is the same: transparency protects you.

Categories of NIL deals that typically require extra scrutiny:

  • Deals with boosters or booster-affiliated entities. These carry the highest compliance risk and are most likely to be scrutinized by the NCAA and your school.
  • Deals with gambling companies. Most schools prohibit athletes from promoting sports gambling brands. This restriction exists even in states where sports betting is legal.
  • Deals that reference your team or school affiliation. You cannot use your school's trademarks, logos, or institutional branding without separate authorization from the school.
  • Deals that could be construed as pay-for-play. If the deal is contingent on your athletic performance or is offered only because of a specific recruitment decision, it likely crosses a line.

When in doubt: ask your compliance office. That's what they're there for. An ounce of disclosure is worth a pound of eligibility reinstatement petition.

Contracts: Read What You Sign

I've seen athletes sign NIL agreements without reading them. I understand the excitement of getting paid to do something you love, but contracts have real consequences.

Things to look for before signing any NIL agreement:

  • Exclusivity clauses. Are you prohibited from working with competitors? For how long? In what categories?
  • Content approval rights. Does the brand have approval authority over what you post? Can they require revisions indefinitely?
  • Term and termination provisions. How long is the deal? Can either party walk away, and under what conditions?
  • Intellectual property ownership. Who owns the content you create? Can the brand use your image after the deal ends?
  • Morality clauses. These are now standard. Understand what behavior could trigger termination.

If the deal is significant—anything over a few thousand dollars, or with a major brand—pay for an attorney to review it. The cost of legal review is far lower than the cost of a bad contract.

The Long Game

The athletes who will look back on NIL positively are the ones who treated it as a business, not a windfall. They built real audiences, chose partnerships that aligned with their values, understood their tax obligations, and kept their compliance office in the loop.

NIL is genuinely a remarkable opportunity. For the first time in the history of college sports, athletes can be compensated for the value they create. Use that opportunity well.
