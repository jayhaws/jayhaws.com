What the House v. NCAA Settlement Means for College Athletes

NIL & Compliance By Jay Haws June 15, 2026 8 min read

When Judge Claudia Wilken gave final approval to the House v. NCAA settlement in the fall of 2024, college sports changed permanently. Not incrementally—permanently. The settlement didn't just resolve a class action lawsuit. It dismantled the foundational legal fiction that had propped up the NCAA's amateurism model for more than a century.

As someone working in athletics compliance and studying sports law at Wake Forest, I've spent a lot of time thinking through what this settlement actually means for athletes navigating the system right now. This post is my attempt to translate the legal and financial complexity into something useful for student-athletes, coaches, and administrators.

What Is House v. NCAA?

House v. NCAA was a class action antitrust lawsuit brought by former college athletes— led by swimmer Grant House—who argued that the NCAA's restrictions on athlete compensation violated federal antitrust law. Specifically, the plaintiffs claimed that athletes were entitled to a share of the revenue their labor generated for schools and conferences, but had been unlawfully denied that share under the guise of "amateurism."

The case built on the legal framework established by the Supreme Court's unanimous 2021 ruling in NCAA v. Alston, which held that the NCAA was not immune from antitrust scrutiny. House pushed further: it wasn't just about education-related benefits. It was about pay.

The settlement resolved the case for approximately $2.8 billion in damages paid out over ten years, and—more importantly for the future—established a framework for schools to share revenue directly with athletes going forward.

The Back Pay Component

The settlement created a damages class covering current and former Division I athletes who competed during a specific damages window. If you were an eligible Division I athlete during that period and you competed in a sport that generated broadcast revenue, you may be entitled to a portion of the damages fund.

The allocation formula is complex—it accounts for sport, division level, and years of participation—but the key point is this: the damages are real, the pool is substantial, and many athletes who are eligible either don't know about the claim or haven't submitted one.

The most underappreciated part of the settlement isn't the revenue-sharing structure going forward. It's the back-pay damages owed to athletes who were competing before NIL existed and received nothing for rights they had.

The Revenue Sharing Framework

Going forward, the settlement creates a mechanism for Division I schools to share revenue directly with their athletes. Schools can now pay athletes approximately $20.5 million per year (a figure that will increase over time), distributed across their rosters.

This is not a salary. It's not a contract. But it functions like one—and the legal and tax implications of that are still being worked out in real time. A few key points:

  • Schools are not required to pay the full cap amount—it's a ceiling, not a floor. Market forces and institutional choices will determine actual distributions.
  • Revenue sharing is separate from NIL. NIL deals with third parties remain a distinct channel. Revenue sharing comes from the school itself.
  • Not all sports will benefit equally. Schools have discretion over how to allocate revenue across sports, and the economics favor football and basketball programs that generate the most revenue.
  • The tax treatment of revenue-sharing payments is unsettled. The IRS has issued some guidance, but athletes should speak with a tax professional before assuming these payments are treated the same as scholarship aid.

What Athletes Should Do Right Now

If you are or were a Division I athlete, there are several concrete steps worth taking:

  1. Check your eligibility for the damages class. The settlement website has a claims portal. If you competed during the covered period, you may be entitled to back pay. Don't leave money on the table.
  2. Understand your school's revenue-sharing policy. Schools are developing these frameworks now. Know what your school is planning to distribute, how it will be allocated, and what the terms look like.
  3. Talk to a tax professional. Both NIL income and revenue-sharing payments are taxable. Many athletes are underprepared for the tax obligations that come with this income.
  4. Get legal representation before signing anything. Revenue-sharing agreements will evolve into more formal contractual arrangements over time. Don't sign without understanding what you're agreeing to.
  5. Stay engaged with your compliance office. The rules are still being written. Athletics compliance professionals can be a valuable resource for understanding what's allowed and what creates risk.

The Bigger Picture

The House settlement is not the end of the legal battles over college athlete compensation. It's arguably the beginning of a new phase. Congress has been discussing federal legislation to create a uniform national framework for college athlete rights. Several states have passed or are considering athlete employment bills. And the antitrust pressure on the NCAA continues.

What's clear is that the old model—where the NCAA could simply declare athletes amateurs and use that label to deprive them of compensation—is over. The question now is what replaces it. That question is going to be answered in courts, in legislatures, in negotiations between conferences and athlete advocates, and ultimately in the contracts that athletes sign with their schools.

That's where sports lawyers will be essential. And it's why I'm paying close attention.
