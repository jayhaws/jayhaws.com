What Coaching Baseball Taught Me About Everything Else

Teaching someone else how to do something you love is a completely different and more demanding skill than doing it yourself.

Baseball June 3, 2026

I started coaching baseball in 2021 because I loved the game and had time on my hands. I kept coaching because I discovered something I wasn't expecting: that teaching someone else how to do something you love is a completely different and more demanding skill than doing it yourself.

Over the next several years I coached players from age six all the way through collegiate summer ball. I gave private lessons to kids who had never held a bat. I managed a collegiate summer team of players my own age, many of whom were better athletes than me, while still suiting up and playing alongside them. I invested my own money into a program that had been underperforming for years and tried to turn it around. I failed more than I succeeded, and I learned more than I expected from both.

The Six-Year-Old Who Didn't Want to Be There

My favorite story from those early years involves a kid I'll call Marcus. He was six years old, and his dad had just bought him a ten-lesson package with me. Before the first session, his dad pulled me aside and said (and I remember this clearly): "He's not fully into it, but he's a decent athlete."

The first two sessions were rough. Marcus was looking everywhere except at me. He had no interest in swinging a bat, no interest in talking about baseball, no interest in being there at all. I've worked with enough kids to know that forcing it only makes it worse. So I stopped forcing it.

On the third session, between rounds in the cages, I started asking him questions, just trying to find something he'd actually talk to me about. I pried it out of him eventually: he'd just watched The Rookie, the Dennis Quaid one about Jim Morris. His eyes lit up in a way they hadn't in our first two hours together. We talked about it. I told him about other baseball movies. Then I said, "Hey, let's take a few swings and we can keep talking after." He agreed, stepped in, and had the best round we'd had in three sessions.

Before the next lesson his dad found me and said: "I don't know what you did, but he's been asking me to play catch in the backyard every night this week and he's watching every baseball movie he can find."

By the end of the ten lessons, Marcus was crushing it on his team and had found a genuine love for the game. I didn't teach him how to hit that week. I found out what he cared about and met him there. The hitting came after.

That lesson has stayed with me in every coaching relationship I've had since and, honestly, in every professional relationship too.

Running a Collegiate Summer Team at 20 Years Old

By 2022 I had taken ownership and management of a collegiate summer ball team in Minnesota, the kind of program where college players come home for the summer and stay sharp while waiting to go back to school. I was still playing. I was also the coach, the GM, the fundraiser, and the guy responsible for making sure we had enough players to field a lineup.

In my first year running the program I added 17 players, got the team new uniforms, and we finished with the best record the program had seen in 21 years. We made the state tournament for the first time in 15 years. I was 20 years old and learning what it meant to be responsible for something beyond myself.

The fundraising piece was its own education. When you're investing your own money into a program at that age, you get creative. I built relationships with local businesses, organized events, and learned that the ask is always easier when you've already demonstrated that you're serious about what you're building. We kept the program financially afloat and I kept my wallet from completely emptying out, which felt like its own kind of victory.

What Coaching Actually Teaches You

I'm now coaching the Wake Forest Law School softball team, which is about as far from collegiate summer ball as you can get and somehow just as rewarding. The players are different. The stakes are different. The underlying work is identical.

The best coaching, I've come to believe, is fundamentally about communication. Not instruction. Communication. There's a difference. Instruction is one directional. Communication means you're reading the person in front of you, understanding what they need in that moment, and adjusting your approach accordingly. Marcus needed a conversation about movies before he needed a hitting lesson. Every athlete I've worked with has had their version of that.

The other thing coaching teaches you is patience with process. You don't build a winning program in one season. You don't turn around a struggling team in one practice. You make small improvements consistently, you build trust slowly, and eventually the results follow. That's not a baseball insight. That's just how most worthwhile things work.

I didn't know when I started coaching six-year-olds in a batting cage in Minnesota that I'd end up in law school writing about antitrust concerns in collegiate athletics. But I'm not sure I would have gotten here without it. Coaching taught me how to lead, how to communicate, and how to stay patient with a process that doesn't always show results on your timeline.

Those turn out to be useful skills in law school too.

Jay Haws is a JD/MBA candidate at Wake Forest University School of Law, Class of 2027. He played D3 baseball and football and coached baseball at multiple levels from 2021 through 2024, including managing and playing for a collegiate summer team in Minnesota.
