What I'm Learning at Wake Forest Athletics: And Why It's Not What I Expected

Compliance work is quieter than the headlines suggest. Here's what it actually looks like from the inside.

Sports Law June 3, 2026

When I tell people I'm spending my summer as an Athletics Compliance extern at Wake Forest, the response is usually some version of "oh so you're learning a lot about NIL?" And the honest answer is: sort of, but not in the way most people think.

Compliance work is quieter than the headlines suggest. It's spreadsheets, regulations, eligibility tracking, and a lot of careful reading of NCAA rules that most people never think about until something goes wrong. I came into this externship having spent the last year as President of Wake Forest's Sports and Entertainment Law Society, having written a 25-page law journal comment on antitrust concerns within the House v. NCAA settlement, and having been invited to a speaker dinner the night before the Journal's NIL Symposium this past spring to sit with practitioners and help facilitate conversation as a local voice on the topics. I thought I knew the space reasonably well.

I did not know the space as well as I thought.

The MBA Is Getting More Reps Than the JD

Here's something nobody tells you about compliance work at the Division I level: it is as much financial modeling and data management as it is legal analysis. My days have involved building models that track eligibility and financial aid changes across an entire athletic roster. When you have hundreds of student-athletes, each with their own academic standing, scholarship status, transfer history, and competition clock, the margin for error is essentially zero.

That's where my Finance undergraduate background and MBA coursework has been surprisingly relevant. Understanding how to build a tracking model that flags exceptions automatically, how to structure data so that a compliance officer can see problems before they become violations: that's not something they teach in Evidence or Contracts. It lives at the intersection of law and operations, and I think that intersection is where I'm most naturally comfortable.

The Gambling Question Is More Complicated Than You Think

One of the more interesting areas I've been paying attention to this summer is how incoming gambling legislation intersects with NCAA eligibility rules. The Brendan Sorsby situation at Texas Tech was something our office was watching closely, trying to get a read on what kind of landscape we'd be navigating and how to explain it to our athletes. It raised more questions than it answered. Fortunately he declared for the draft before it played out further, but the underlying tension between institutional enforcement and athlete welfare isn't going anywhere.

The NCAA has rules. States have laws. Those laws are not uniform. And student-athletes, many of whom are 18 to 22 years old, are navigating this environment without always understanding where the lines are. Part of what our staff does is proactive education: making sure athletes understand the rules before a situation arises rather than responding after the fact. I've been involved in building that kind of communication infrastructure this summer and it's some of the most practically meaningful work I've done in law school.

Why This Externship Matters to Me Beyond the Resume Line

I've been having conversations with people in collegiate and professional athletics for the last year and a half. Coaches, compliance officers, agents, front office staff. Those conversations kept pointing me toward the same conclusion: the people who are most effective in sports law are the ones who understand both the legal framework and the operational reality of how athletic departments actually function day to day.

This externship is giving me that operational view in real time. Not through a case study or a law review article, but by sitting inside the department while decisions are being made and understanding the pressure points that don't make it into any textbook.

Whether my path leads to a firm with a sports practice group, in-house with a professional franchise, or somewhere else entirely, I don't know yet. What I do know is that the combination of legal training, financial modeling, and genuine love for sports that has defined most of my life so far is not a liability in this field. It might be exactly the point.

Jay Haws is a JD/MBA candidate at Wake Forest University School of Law, Class of 2027. He is currently an Athletics Compliance extern at Wake Forest Athletics and a former President of the Sports and Entertainment Law Society.
